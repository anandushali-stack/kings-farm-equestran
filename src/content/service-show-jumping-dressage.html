The natural gaits of the horse are the walk, the trot, the canter or slow gallop, and the gallop, although in dressage the canter and gallop are not usually differentiated. A riding horse is trained in each gait and in the change from one to another.

During the walk and the gallop the horse's head moves down and forward, then up and back (only at the trot is it still); riders follow these movements with their hands. Understanding these movements is fundamental to becoming a skilled rider.

Understanding Each Gait

A horse's physical development, its mental understanding of what is being asked of it, and the ease with which it potentially relates to the human can be sought after in any riding style or discipline.

Walk

The walk is a slow, four-beat, rhythmic pace of distinct successive hoof beats. It may be a free, collected, or extended walk.

Trot

The trot is a two-beat gait, light and balanced. Riders can either sit or rise (post) to the trot.

Canter, Gallop & Beyond

As the horse moves faster, its gait changes into the canter, a three-beat gait, graceful and elegant. An accelerated canter becomes the gallop, in which the rider's weight is brought sharply forward as the horse reaches speeds up to 30 miles (48 kilometres) an hour.

  • Walk: slow, four-beat rhythmic pace
  • Trot: two-beat gait, light and balanced
  • Canter: three-beat gait, graceful and elegant
  • Gallop: accelerated canter, up to 48 km/h
  • Other gaits: rack, pace, fox trot, amble

The natural gaits of the horse are the walk, the trot, the canter or slow gallop, and the gallop. Each gait has its own rhythm and characteristics that riders learn to feel and follow.

Riders can rise to the trot (post) by rising out of the saddle slightly and allowing more of their weight to bear on the stirrups when one or the other of the diagonal pairs of legs leaves the ground. Posting reduces the impact of the trot on both horse and rider.
